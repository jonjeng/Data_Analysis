Introduction¶

This dataset (noshowappointments) consists of recorded information on 110,527 medical appointments at public hospitals in Brazil. The data includes:

  • the day and time the appointment was made,
  • the day and time for which the appointment was scheduled,
  • the neighborhood in which the hospital is located,
  • the patient's age,
  • the patient's gender,
  • whether the patient has received a scholarship (a subsidy for low-income families that meet certain requirements, such as having children going to school),
  • whether the patient has hypertension,
  • whether the patient has diabetes,
  • whether the patient has alcoholism,
  • whether the patient has a handicap,
  • whether the patient was given an SMS reminder, and
  • whether the patient attended the appointment

In this report, I plan to explore the relationship between each given detail and whether or not the patient attended the appointment. I would also like to derive an additional detail - the time between when the appointment was scheduled and the time of the appointment, and see how much this relates with attendance.

I'd also like to see if any of the given health conditions and/or age correlate with a likelihood of making an appointment, as well as if any neighborhoods have a greater or fewer number of patients with the given health conditions

def bar(xdata, ydata, title_text):
    """
    This function creates a barchart with xdata on the x-axis and y-data on the y-axis. The visualization is shown
      using plt.show() so as to avoid outputting distracting text.
    
    Inputs:
      xdata : a 1D group of numbers of the same size as ydata
      ydata : a 1D group of numbers of the same size as xdata
    Output:
      None
    """
    
    # First, we use the barplot() function in the Seaborn library with the given arguments and store the result as ax.
    ax = sns.barplot(x=xdata, y=ydata)
    
    # Set the title as per the given argument and y-axis label appropriately.
    plt.title(title_text, fontsize=16)
    plt.ylabel('Frequency')
    
    # Set the x-axis tick labels to informative values
    ax.set(xticklabels=['No','Yes'])
    
    return ax

Of note, however, is the fact that Pearson correlation is meaningful only for linear relationships between, while Spearman correlation attempts to address nonlinearity but is meaningful for only monotonic relationships. Thus, the obtained values should be taken with caution and we will attempt visual inspection of relationships.

def freq_table(col, ind):
    """
    This function creates a relative frequency table by use of the crosstab() function in pandas. The relevant
      categories are the column names of df indicated by the argument col and the 'No-show' column. 
      Ind gives the names of the indices of the table.
    Inputs:
      col : the name of a column of df
      ind : a list of strings to which the index should be assigned
    Output:
      relevant cells of the table, not including the cells corresponding to totals.
    """
    freq_table = pd.crosstab(index=df[col], columns=df['No-show'], margins=True)
    
    # Set column names appropriately
    freq_table.columns = ['Attended', 'Did Not Attend', 'Total']
    
    # Remove the row corresponding to column totals because we won't need it
    freq_table = freq_table.iloc[:-1]
    
    # Set the index to ind
    freq_table.index = ind
    
    # Divide by the values with corresponding indices in the 'Total' column and multiply the quotient by 100 to obtain
    #  relative frequencies
    freq_table = 100 * freq_table.div(freq_table['Total'], axis=0)
    
    return freq_table[['Attended', 'Did Not Attend']]

Conclusions¶

Based on patient gender and age, as well as the prevalence of appointment non-attendance, the data does not seem representative, neither of the Brazilian population nor of the totality of medical appointments in Brazil. Of course, such a conclusion is not definite, as it may well be the case that vastly fewer males visit hospitals and about 20% of all appointments are not attended. The ages of patients in the sample seem a bit odd, though.

Next, we investigated univariate relationships between the given information and appointment attendance. The following variables were shown to have somewhat of a relationship with attendance and thus may have some predictive power:

  • Age
  • Scholarship
  • Hypertension (hipertension)
  • Diabetes
  • Handicap (handcap)
  • SMS received

A relationship between attendance and the following variables was not found:

  • Gender
  • Scheduled month
  • Scheduled hour
  • Appointment day
  • Neighborhood
  • Alcoholism

Curiously, the receiving of SMS seemed to correlate negatively with appointment attendance. If the messages are reminders then they are counterproductive. Further, with regard to all of the discovered relationships, neither directionality of the relationships nor whether the relationships were causal could be determined.

Lastly, we found that attended appointments tended to have a lower difference between the appointment day and scheduled day. Most appointments (about 79.7%) were attended but even with this disparity, if there is a great difference between appointment day and scheduled day, it was less likely that the appointment day.

Unfortunately, all of the aforementioned relationships were slight at best, representing a deviation of less than three percent from the general attendance ratio of approximately 79.7%. Given that the plausible relationships are very weak and considering that the sample does not seem representative of the population (i.e., neither that of Brazil nor of medical appointments in Brazil), it seems dubious that attendance can be predicted from the given information at a rate well above random selection using the approximately 79.7% attendance rate. This investigation did not consider multivariate relationships, apart from attendance in relationship to another variable. These may exist, but it seems that more data (e.g., additional records) should be collected first.
